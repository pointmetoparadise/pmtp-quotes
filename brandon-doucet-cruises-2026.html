About the cabin selections

You'll notice every cabin in this quote is on Deck 6 — that's deliberate. I pulled the lowest balcony deck available on each ship. Lower decks sit closer to the ship's center of gravity, which translates to less motion underfoot if seas get bumpy. "Low" is also relative on a cruise ship: balconies can't be anywhere near the waterline, so Deck 6 is still well above the ocean, with mechanical decks and crew areas tucked underneath.

— About Symphony of the Seas —

An Oasis-class megaship in a category of its own

Symphony of the Seas is one of Royal Caribbean's largest ships — part of the Oasis class, the line's flagship category. The Mariner is a perfectly good ship for getting you to Cozumel and back. Symphony is something different: a ship designed so that the days at sea are as much the point as the days in port.

The architecture splits the ship into seven distinct "neighborhoods," each with its own atmosphere:

Central ParkAn open-air promenade with real trees, restaurants, and live music in the evenings.
The BoardwalkA Coney-Island-style street with a hand-carved carousel, the AquaTheater, and Johnny Rockets.
Royal PromenadeThe interior main street — bars, shops, parades, and the multi-deck atrium.
Pool & Sports ZoneMultiple pools, twin FlowRider surf simulators, a zip line, mini-golf, and an ice-skating rink.
Vitality Spa & FitnessFull spa, salon, and a serious gym.
Entertainment DistrictBroadway-caliber theater (Hairspray runs onboard), comedy club, jazz lounge, casino.

Plus the headline pieces that aren't really "neighborhoods" — the Ultimate Abyss (a ten-story dry slide), the AquaTheater high-diving shows, and a Solarium adults-only deck. Three ports, two sea days, and a ship that can absorb six nights without feeling repetitive.

— two lines, two atmospheres —

Royal Caribbean vs. Carnival

Both lines run good ships. They feel different on board. If you've never sailed both, here's the honest read.
Royal Caribbean
Polished, family-broad, entertainment-heavy
  • Larger ships across the fleet — especially Oasis-class (Symphony, Wonder, Icon)
  • Higher production-value entertainment: Broadway shows, ice skating, AquaTheater on the bigger ships
  • Dining feels slightly more polished; specialty restaurants run $25–65 per person
  • Service is consistent and attentive
  • Drink packages and Wi-Fi are noticeably more expensive than Carnival
  • Crowd skews multi-generational and family-broad — more couples, fewer bachelor parties
Carnival
Casual, lively, value-focused
  • Mid-size to large ships — none reach Oasis-class scale
  • Stronger comedy programming, big casino floors, more party-friendly atmosphere
  • Food is hearty and abundant — Guy Fieri's burger joint, BlueIguana cantina, RedFrog Pub all included
  • Drink packages and add-ons price out lower than Royal Caribbean
  • Service is friendly and warm, slightly less formal
  • Crowd skews younger and more casual — louder energy, especially on shorter sailings

Neither line is "better" — they're built for different vacation moods. Royal Caribbean tends to feel like a polished resort at sea. Carnival tends to feel like a really good party with great food.

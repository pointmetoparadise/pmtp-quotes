A Note Before We Begin

The Philosophy Behind This Trip

You asked for an Amalfi that feels less touristy, and for the Greek Islands "done right." Both are possible — but they require a few honest conversations most advisors skip.

The Amalfi Coast is famous because it's small. Thirty miles of coastline. A handful of villages. One winding road. There's no undiscovered Amalfi — but there's a meaningful difference between the Amalfi the day-trippers experience and the one the residents know. That difference isn't about finding secret villages. It's about choosing a base that filters the crowds out, timing activities to the rhythm locals use, and experiencing the coast from the water whenever possible.

Greece asks a different question. Most couples assume more islands means a better trip. The math usually disagrees. Every ferry day, every check-in, every airport transfer is a day you're moving instead of being there. For an anniversary trip especially, depth tends to beat breadth.

"The hardest thing about planning Europe isn't picking where to go. It's resisting the urge to add one more place."

The page below walks through the real decisions. Each section gives you honest context, then asks you to mark what resonates. Your selections feed a summary panel at the end, which you'll send back to me with any notes. From there we firm up pricing, pace, and all the logistics.

Question One

How Do You Want Italy to Begin?

Rome is magnificent, but it's also its own trip. Three days in Rome gives you the Vatican, the Colosseum, one memorable dinner in Trastevere, and very little time for anything else. The alternative is to skip it entirely this trip — fly into Naples, drive straight to the coast, and let the whole Italy leg be about slowing down.

Amalfi Deep Dive

7 nights on the coast, no detours

Fly into Naples, drive straight to your Amalfi home base, and stay put. The trip becomes about one place, experienced deeply.

  • Maximum time on the coast
  • One unpacking, no transfers
  • Room for slow mornings, long boat days, and unhurried dinners
  • Ideal if you've done Rome before or prefer single-destination trips

Rome First, Then Coast

3 nights Rome + 4 nights Amalfi

Land in Rome, spend three days with the essentials, then take the train south and transfer to the coast for four nights.

  • Vatican, Colosseum, and one great Trastevere night
  • High-speed train Rome → Naples (about 70 minutes), then private transfer to the coast
  • Trade-off: fewer full days in Amalfi
  • Best if it's your first time in Italy, or Rome is on the bucket list
Question Two

Your Amalfi Home Base

The Amalfi Coast is a string of villages, each with its own character. Where you wake up each morning shapes the entire trip. Here are five legitimate choices — read each, then mark the one that feels most like yours. You can always change your mind.

Christina's Lead Recommendation

Ravello

The cliffside queen — above the crowds, literally

Ravello sits roughly 1,200 feet above the sea on a separate cliff from the coastal towns. The drive up filters out the day-trippers. What you get in return is a UNESCO-listed village of stone lanes, medieval villas, and gardens that have drawn artists and composers — Wagner, Gore Vidal, Jackie Kennedy — for more than a century. Mornings are quiet. Evenings are quieter. And because you're 5–8 degrees cooler than the coast, September here is honestly pleasant.

The trade-off: no walk-out-your-door beach. You'll descend to the water by private boat or driver — which is actually how the locals prefer to see the coast anyway.

Hotel Scene Palazzo Avino, Belmond Hotel Caruso, and Hotel Villa Cimbrone — three of the most celebrated anniversary properties in Italy. All five-star. Rooms book 6–9 months out for September.
Pros
  • 1,200 ft elevation filters out day-trippers
  • 5–8°F cooler than coastal towns in September
  • UNESCO-listed; Villa Rufolo & Villa Cimbrone gardens, classical music festival
  • Three top-tier anniversary 5-stars (Palazzo Avino, Belmond Caruso, Hotel Villa Cimbrone)
Cons
  • No walk-out beach — must descend ~200 steps or take a car/boat to the water
  • Need driver or boat for every excursion
  • Highest price tier on the coast, especially the flagship hotels
Quiet Alternative

Praiano

Between the hotspots, facing the sunset

Praiano sits on the road between Positano and Amalfi Town, which means you watch the coastline's two most famous villages from a distance while staying somewhere that still feels like a real fishing village. The geography matters: Praiano faces west, so you get the Amalfi Coast's best sunsets — something Positano itself doesn't have, because it faces east.

It's small, walkable, and has a handful of excellent restaurants. The pace is unhurried. You're 15 minutes from Positano by water taxi when you want the scene, and 10 minutes from Amalfi when you want the history.

Hotel Scene Casa Angelina — a 37-room adults-only design hotel with a minimalist white aesthetic and a rooftop restaurant that catches the full sunset. A frequent anniversary choice.
Pros
  • Faces west — the coast's best sunsets (Positano itself faces east)
  • Between Positano and Amalfi without the crowd density of either
  • Casa Angelina is adults-only, 39 rooms, known for rooftop dining
  • Small fishing-village character preserved
Cons
  • Limited 5-star inventory (essentially Casa Angelina at that tier)
  • Fewer restaurants than Positano or Amalfi
  • Cliff-built — expect stairs and elevators to reach the water
  • Not walkable to Positano or Amalfi — water taxi or car
Hotel-As-Destination

Conca dei Marini

A tiny fishing hamlet, defined by one hotel

Conca dei Marini is barely a village — a cluster of houses on a promontory between Amalfi and Positano. You don't come here for the town. You come for Monastero Santa Rosa: a 17th-century Dominican monastery converted into a 20-suite hotel with a Michelin-starred restaurant, four levels of terraced gardens, and a cliff-edge infinity pool that is one of the most photographed in Italy.

This is the choice when the hotel is the trip. You don't wander into town for dinner — you don't particularly need to. A complimentary shuttle handles trips to Amalfi; a private boat handles everything else.

Hotel Scene Monastero Santa Rosa Hotel & Spa — adults-only, historic, and consistently on "world's best" lists. The experience is inseparable from the property.
Pros
  • Tiny fishing hamlet — essentially zero day-trippers
  • Monastero Santa Rosa is a former 13th-c. Dominican convent with a Michelin-starred restaurant
  • Iconic cliffside infinity pool, four levels of terraced gardens
  • Adults-only; complimentary shuttle to Amalfi town
Cons
  • Village has no dining or shopping scene — the hotel is the destination
  • Essentially one luxury property; limited alternatives
  • Best for hotel-based vacations, not for couples who want to wander village life
Historical Heart

Amalfi Town

The town that gave the coast its name

Amalfi is the historical heart of the whole coast — a 9th-century maritime republic whose name became the label for the entire stretch of coastline. The 11th-century cathedral, reached by a dramatic flight of stone steps from the main piazza, is the coast's most important historical monument. Unlike Ravello (up the cliff) or Positano (clinging to it), Amalfi sits on the water — ferries to Capri, Positano, and Sorrento leave from its marina, making it one of the most logistically central bases on the coast.

Two serious 5-star anchors here. Hotel Santa Caterina has been family-owned by the Gambardella family since 1880 and is one of the grande dames of Italian hospitality — cliffside, with a Michelin-starred restaurant (Glicine) and an elevator cut into the rock descending to a private sea-level beach club. Anantara Convento di Amalfi Grand Hotel — a 13th-century Capuchin convent fully renovated and rebranded in 2023 — sits on a promontory five minutes' walk from the Piazza del Duomo, with a cliff-edge infinity pool.

Hotel Scene Hotel Santa Caterina (67 rooms, family-owned since 1880, 1-Michelin-star restaurant) and Anantara Convento di Amalfi Grand Hotel (52 suites, former 13th-c. Capuchin convent, renovated 2023).
Pros
  • Historical and logistical center of the coast — UNESCO World Heritage
  • Ferry hub — direct boats to Capri, Positano, Sorrento, Salerno
  • On the water, not clinging above it — own beach, less steep than Positano
  • Two legitimate anniversary-caliber 5-stars
  • 10-minute walk to Atrani (smallest town in Italy)
Cons
  • Main piazza draws cruise-ship day-trippers 11am–4pm
  • Less visually iconic than Positano's pastel-cliff postcard
  • Narrow, busy streets in the old town center
  • Santa Caterina is a 15-minute uphill walk back from town (shuttle available)
The Iconic Choice

Positano

The postcard — crowds, charm, and the most famous view in Italy

I'd be dishonest to skip it. Positano is the Amalfi Coast you see in every photograph: pastel houses tumbling down a cliff, boats bobbing in a crescent harbor, bougainvillea spilling from every terrace. It has the coast's best shopping, its most famous beach clubs, and its most glamorous nightlife.

It also has the crowds. Between 11am and 4pm in peak season, the main pedestrian stairways are shoulder-to-shoulder with day-trippers off ferries and cruise ships. The honest play: stay here, but time your days around locals' rhythms — beach early, village quiet time during lunch, come back out after 5pm when the tour buses leave.

Hotel Scene Le Sirenuse (iconic, family-run), Il San Pietro (on its own bluff just outside town, private and quiet), Villa TreVille (former home of Franco Zeffirelli, just a few suites).
Pros
  • The coast's most iconic townscape — the postcard in person
  • Best shopping, beach clubs, and evening scene
  • Legendary hotels: Le Sirenuse, Il San Pietro (on its own bluff), Villa TreVille
  • Spiaggia Grande beach right in town
Cons
  • Heavy day-tripper crowds 11am–4pm in season
  • Faces east — no sunset view from Positano itself
  • Extremely steep — significant stair-climbing to get anywhere
  • Highest hotel rates on the Amalfi Coast
Gateway Option

Sorrento

Not technically Amalfi — but it has advantages

Sorrento sits on the other side of the peninsula — facing Naples and Vesuvius rather than Amalfi. It has better road access (no hairpin mountain driving), better ferry connections (direct boats to Capri, Positano, Amalfi), and more dining and hotel variety. It's also a real, working Italian town, not a tourism-only village.

The trade-off: you're not on the Amalfi Coast. Most days become "go somewhere else" — Capri, Positano, Pompeii. It's a hub, not a home base. For some couples that's the right structure; for others it defeats the point.

Hotel Scene Grand Hotel Excelsior Vittoria (clifftop, family-run since 1834) or Bellevue Syrene (a villa turned hotel on the cliff with direct sea elevator). Both deeply romantic and established.
Pros
  • Flat town — far fewer stairs than any Amalfi Coast village
  • Best transport hub — direct ferries to Capri, Positano, Amalfi, Naples
  • Greater hotel and restaurant variety at every tier
  • Easier road access (no winding Amalfi Drive to arrive)
Cons
  • Not technically on the Amalfi Coast — faces Naples, not Amalfi
  • Most days become "go somewhere else" — a hub, not a home base
  • Larger, more town-like feel; less romantic atmosphere
  • You won't wake up to Amalfi Coast views

For Completeness

Other Towns on the Amalfi Coast

The six options above are the realistic anniversary bases. For completeness, here are five more Amalfi Coast towns worth knowing about — each interesting in its own right, but with limitations at the luxury tier. If any of these pull you more than the options above, tell me in the notes and we'll discuss.

Atrani

The smallest town in Italy — 12 hectares, ~830 residents

A preserved medieval fishing village a 10-minute walk from Amalfi. One of the best-preserved medieval townscapes on the entire coast, often cited on lists of Italy's most beautiful villages.

Pros: authentic, quiet, genuine village feel, pebble beach

Cons: no 5-star hotels — mostly B&Bs and apartments (Palazzo Ferraioli, 4-star, is the top property)

Minori

The "City of Taste"

A quieter village east of Amalfi, known for its food culture — home of Sal de Riso, one of Italy's most celebrated pastry chefs. Ferry connections to the rest of the coast.

Pros: authentic local feel, excellent food, more affordable, less crowded

Cons: primarily 3- and 4-star hotels; limited luxury inventory

Maiori

The longest beach on the Amalfi Coast

A slightly larger coastal town next to Minori, distinguished by the longest continuous beach on the Amalfi Coast — rare, since most of the coast has only tiny pebble coves.

Pros: wide sandy beach, more affordable, good ferry access, Hotel Botanico San Lazzaro (5-star)

Cons: more beach-town than dramatic clifftop; less "iconic Amalfi" visual drama

Cetara

The coast's authentic fishing village

Described by travel writers as "immune to mass tourism." Famous for colatura di alici (a DOP-recognized anchovy sauce dating to Roman times) and tuna caught with traditional methods.

Pros: most authentic working fishing village on the coast, serious seafood

Cons: very limited hotel inventory — no luxury properties; primarily B&Bs and small seaside residences

Vietri sul Mare

The eastern gateway — Italy's ceramics capital

The easternmost Amalfi Coast town, synonymous with Italian ceramics since the 1600s. Famous for polychrome majolica pottery; every shop window shows it off.

Pros: unique ceramics shopping, easier access from Naples/Salerno, wide sandy beach

Cons: feels more Salerno-adjacent than "Amalfi Coast proper"; Hotel Raito (4–5 star) is the leading option

Before We Talk Islands

Depth vs. Breadth in Greece

Here's where most advisors fail their clients: they give you five islands on one trip and call it "comprehensive." What you actually get is five ferry days, five check-ins, and a blur of places you'll struggle to distinguish in photos a year later.

Every move costs vacation time. A ferry day is a lost beach day. An early flight is a lost slow morning.

For a 14–15 night trip split between Italy and Greece, here's the real math:

One Island, Done Right

1 night Athens + 5–6 nights island. You actually settle in. You have a favorite taverna by night three. You come home with a place, not a highlight reel.

Two Islands, Split

1 night Athens + 3 + 3 or 4. Two tastes, two check-ins, at least one ferry day. You trade depth for variety — and one full day to transit.

Three+ Islands

Don't. Not on this trip. Not on an anniversary. For that pace you need closer to three weeks of island time alone.

A pattern we see often: couples who pick one island and give it real time come home happier than those who did two. But if there's a specific reason to want two — a bucket-list beach, a restaurant, a photo you've seen — we'll make two work. You know you better than we do.

Question Three

Athens: Yes or No?

You have to land somewhere. The question is whether Athens gets a proper night — with a sunset Acropolis visit and a memorable dinner in Plaka — or whether it's just a connection you push through on the way to the island.

One Night in Athens

+ 1 night, before the island

Land, drop bags at a hotel near Syntagma or Plaka, see the Acropolis at golden hour (when the crowds thin), have one exceptional dinner, fly to the island the next morning rested.

  • You'll have seen the Parthenon — a thing you'll always remember
  • Built-in buffer against flight delays to the island
  • Easier for first-time Greece travelers
  • Trade: one less night on the island

Straight to the Island

Athens is a connection, not a stop

Same-day connection through Athens to the island. You land, change planes, and are in your island hotel by sunset. Every night of Greece is island time.

  • Maximum island time
  • Works best if you've been to Athens before
  • Requires forgiving flight timing (morning arrival into ATH)
  • Trade: no Acropolis on this trip
The Island Scope

Seven Islands Worth Considering

Mykonos is intentionally not below — it's generally considered the wrong energy for an anniversary, and the party-island reputation is accurate rather than overblown. Santorini is here, with honest framing about September crowds. Every other Cycladic island worth an anniversary stay is below. Read through, see what pulls you.

Milos

40-min flight
The island that doesn't look like Greece

Milos is volcanic — white rock formations, lunar beaches like Sarakiniko that feel like another planet, and Kleftiko, a cluster of sea caves reachable only by boat. It has the Cyclades' most distinctive landscape. Luxury hotels arrived relatively recently, so the island still feels authentic.

Known Hotels Milos Cove (42 suites, private beach), Domes White Coast Milos, Skinopi Lodge (minimalist, intimate)
Character: Dramatic volcanic landscape, swim spots, boat culture

Paros

40-min flight
Chic, walkable, polished

Paros has quietly become one of the Cyclades' most stylish islands. Naoussa is a harbor village of whitewashed chapels and waterfront tavernas that comes alive at dinner. Beaches are soft sand. The food scene is serious. The experience is more "designed" than wild.

Known Hotels Cosme, a Luxury Collection Resort (40 suites on Naoussa Bay), Parilio (architectural, inland), Vione (adults-only)
Character: Restaurants, walkability, refined aesthetic, harbor life

Naxos

45-min flight
The Cyclades' beach island

Naxos has the longest and widest sandy beaches in the Cyclades — the western coast is roughly 40 kilometers of nearly continuous soft golden sand, from organized beaches like Agios Prokopios and Plaka to wild, undeveloped coves at Aliko and Pyrgaki. Most Cycladic islands have lovely but small coves; Naxos has actual stretches of beach. It's also the largest island in the group, with a mountainous green interior of hilltop villages, ancient temples, and a serious local food culture (cheese, honey, meat, olive oil).

Known Hotels More villa-rental territory than big-brand resort territory. Naxian Collection, Tinomoti, and a number of boutique beachfront properties along the Agios Prokopios / Plaka coastline.
Character: Sand, space, mountain villages, authentic local life
Trade: Fewer high-end anniversary hotel options than Paros or Milos

Sifnos

Ferry only
The culinary capital of the Cyclades

Sifnos is where Greeks go when they want the best food. The island's tradition of slow-cooked clay-pot cooking (mastelo, revithada) gave Greek cuisine some of its foundational dishes. Beyond the food: pottery villages, cliff-edge chapels, and marked hiking trails between hilltop hamlets.

Known Hotels Verina Astra (minimalist cliffside), Elies Resort (traditional village architecture)
Character: Food-forward, quiet, traditional Cycladic rhythm
Trade: Ferry only (~2.5 hrs from Piraeus on fast boat), which complicates Italy pairing

Folegandros

Ferry only
Romantic through sheer remoteness

Folegandros is what Santorini looked like 40 years ago — a cliffside Chora perched above the sea, almost no cars, hiking trails across wild landscape, and until recently, nowhere luxurious to stay. That changed in 2024 with Gundari, the island's first five-star hotel. It's a genuine "get away from everything" island.

Known Hotels Gundari (27 suites, opened May 2024 — seasonal May–Oct), Anemi
Character: Quiet, raw, cliffside beauty, few beaches
Trade: Ferry-only, no airport. Reached via Milos, Santorini, or a 2.5–4 hour ferry from Athens

Antiparos

Via Paros
The whisper-network island

Antiparos is a 10-minute ferry from Paros — a tiny island with a single main village, a handful of beaches, and an outsized reputation among people who prefer not to share their vacation spots. Tom Hanks has a house here. So does Madonna. You'd never know; the island does discretion well.

Known Hotels The Rooster (Small Luxury Hotels of the World) — a destination-level retreat that's often the entire reason to come
Character: Tiny, private, understated. Typically paired with Paros rather than visited alone

Santorini

45–50 min flight
The famous one — caldera, crowds, and the photo you've seen

Santorini is Santorini. The caldera views are genuinely that remarkable, the white-and-blue villages are genuinely that photogenic, and no other island looks like it. It's also still crowded in September — cruise ships continue to disembark daily, and Oia at sunset is a scrum of phones regardless of season.

The honest workaround: stay in Imerovigli or Pyrgos (not Oia or Fira), pick a hotel with a private caldera view so you experience the famous scenery from your own terrace, photograph Oia at sunrise instead of sunset, and time beach and wine trips around cruise-ship schedules.

Known Hotels Canaves Oia (clifftop, private pools), Grace Hotel Auberge (in Imerovigli), Mystique (Luxury Collection, Oia's quieter end), Vedema (Luxury Collection, in Megalochori near Pyrgos)
Character: Iconic, dramatic, visually unmatched
Trade: September is shoulder season, not low season — expect cruise crowds at the top spots
Or Pick a Pre-Built Shape

Three Sample Trip Structures

If the a-la-carte version feels like too many decisions, here are three combinations that work logistically — each with a very different feel. None is better than the others; they're just different trips.

Option One

Milos, Deeply

1 night Athens + 6 nights Milos

One island, real time. A private boat day to Kleftiko's sea caves. Sarakiniko at sunrise before the day-trippers arrive. Village dinners in Pollonia. The Greek leg becomes about settling somewhere genuinely extraordinary and letting it unfold.

Option Two

Paros, with Style

1 night Athens + 6 nights Paros

Walkable Naoussa dinners, a real food scene, and a destination that's more "designed" than wild. Optional day-trip to Antiparos for lunch at The Rooster. Pool mornings. Sunset harbor walks. Infrastructure and ease throughout.

Option Three

Paros + Folegandros

1 Athens + 4 Paros + 3 Folegandros

Infrastructure on Paros, wilderness on Folegandros. One ferry day between. Requires adding a night overall, or trimming Amalfi to 6. The two-island version if you want genuine contrast between them.

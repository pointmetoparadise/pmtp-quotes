Stop one · Oct 3–9 · 6 nights

Praiano, the Amalfi Coast

Quieter than Positano next door, with the same sea and a fraction of the crowd — choose one to call home for six nights.

Where you'll be. Praiano sits on the Amalfi Coast road roughly halfway between Positano (about 15 minutes west) and Amalfi town (about 15 minutes east), draped down a cliff between the sea and the mountains. It's residential and calm where Positano is a stage set — the same turquoise water and vertical drama, far fewer day-trippers, and prices a notch gentler.

Getting around. The coast runs on the SITA local bus (tickets from most reception desks), water taxis, and private boats; the road is narrow and a car is more burden than help. Praiano is also the trailhead village for the Sentiero degli Dei (Path of the Gods), the cliff walk above the coast. Most of these hotels are family-run and built into the rock, so expect steps — worth weighing if stairs are a concern.

What early October looks like

Early October on the Amalfi Coast is the tail of the warm season — comfortable days, sea still warm enough to swim, softer light, and far thinner crowds than summer. The honest trade: it's the wind-down. Some beach clubs, water taxis, and seasonal restaurants begin reducing hours or closing for winter, and a few hotel pools are seasonal. Many travelers prefer exactly this — the coast at its calmest — but it's a quieter, slower version of Praiano than July, not the peak-season buzz.

Stop two · Oct 9–13 · 4 nights

Paros — Naoussa

A working fishing village turned quietly stylish — whitewashed lanes, a real harbor, far less polished-for-tourists than Santorini.

Where you'll be. Naoussa sits on the north coast of Paros, built around a small fishing harbor where the boats still come in and the tavernas line the water. The old town is a maze of whitewashed lanes, bougainvillea, and boutiques — lively at night, but human-scaled rather than the crush of Mykonos or Santorini's caldera towns.

The beaches. The Kolympithres area (wind-sculpted granite coves) and Santa Maria and Monastiri beaches are a short drive or water-taxi hop around the bay. Your options split between in-town/near-beach (Kanale's, Lilly, Cove on Agioi Anargyroi) and a design retreat set back toward Kolympithres (Parilio, which is a landscape-and-pool hotel rather than a walk-to-the-sand one). Lilly and Cove each appear twice below — two room choices at each of those addresses. Paros is reached by ferry from Naxos/Athens or its own small airport; none of these properties has an elevator.

What mid-October looks like

Mid-October is genuine shoulder-into-off-season in the Cyclades, and it cuts both ways. The upside is real: Naoussa is calm, the light is gorgeous, restaurants are easy to get into, and rates drop. The trade-offs to plan around: ferry schedules thin out (fewer daily crossings — island-hopping needs more planning), the Aegean can turn windy, and some beach tavernas and seasonal hotels begin closing for winter — several Cycladic properties run only May through October. Plenty of travelers love Paros most in October for exactly this quiet; just go in knowing it's the off-season version, not high summer.

Stop three · Oct 13–17 · 4 nights

Santorini

The real choice here isn't which hotel — it's which Santorini. The caldera or the beach. They're two different weeks.
Caldera or beach — the honest trade. The caldera side (Oia, Imerovigli) is the Santorini of the postcards: cliff-edge suites, sunsets over the volcano, plunge pools hanging above the sea. The cost is stairs — these villages climb, and none of the Imerovigli options below has an elevator. One honest detail on the views: across the three Imerovigli properties below, only Afroessa faces the caldera and its sunset — the rooms quoted at Cresanto and West East face east (sea and sunrise), which is calmer and better value but not the postcard sunset. Cresanto and West East each show more than one room option. The beach side (Kamari, Perissa) trades the famous view for black-sand shores you walk straight onto, ground-level access, and lower prices. Neither is wrong — each card states exactly what its room looks out on.
What mid-October looks like

By the time you reach Santorini (Oct 13–17) it's firmly shoulder season. The rewards are the ones repeat travelers chase: the caldera without the summer crush, warm-but-not-scorching days, and softer prices. Plan around the trade-offs — the meltemi winds can pick up, evenings cool off (a light layer for sunset), ferry frequency is reduced, and a number of beach properties and tavernas in Kamari and Perissa wind down or close near the end of the month, since many run only May through October. The caldera villages stay livelier later than the beach towns. October Santorini is quieter and calmer by design — a feature for most couples, as long as you arrive expecting it.

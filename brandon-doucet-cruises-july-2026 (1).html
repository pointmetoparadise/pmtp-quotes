About the cabin selections

You'll notice the cabins skew toward lower-to-mid decks (Decks 6, 7, and 8) — that's deliberate. Lower decks sit closer to a ship's center of gravity, which translates to less motion underfoot if seas get bumpy. "Low" is also relative on a cruise ship: balconies can't be anywhere near the waterline, so even Deck 6 is well above the ocean, with mechanical decks and crew areas tucked underneath.

Royal Caribbean · Voyager-class · Mid-size

Mariner of the Seas

The dependable Royal workhorse — well-amped, balanced, never overwhelming

Mariner is Royal's Voyager-class workhorse for Galveston — mid-sized rather than megasized, carrying about 3,300 guests instead of Symphony's 6,800. After a $120 million refurbishment in 2018, it picked up most of the marquee Royal experiences without the scale: a FlowRider surf simulator, two waterslides (The Perfect Storm), a rock climbing wall, the Sky Pad bungee VR experience, and an ice rink on Deck 3 (Studio B) that converts into a Broadway-style theater.

The Royal Promenade — Royal's signature multi-deck interior boulevard lined with bars, shops, and the multi-deck atrium — runs through the heart of the ship. Specialty dining includes Chops Grille (steakhouse), Giovanni's Table (Italian), and Izumi (sushi). Adults can retreat to the Solarium, an adults-only pool deck with panoramic glass.

Best for: someone who wants a real Royal Caribbean experience — energetic, family-broad, with everything onboard — but doesn't want to wander a megaship for a week.

Royal Caribbean · Oasis-class · Megaship

Symphony of the Seas

An entirely different scale — the ship is the vacation

Symphony is one of Royal Caribbean's largest ships — part of the Oasis class, the line's flagship category, with roughly 6,800 guests at capacity. The Mariner is a perfectly good ship for getting you to Cozumel and back. Symphony is something else entirely: a ship designed so that the days at sea matter as much as the days in port.

The architecture splits the ship into seven distinct "neighborhoods," each with its own atmosphere:

Central ParkAn open-air promenade with real trees, restaurants, and live music in the evenings.
The BoardwalkA Coney-Island-style street with a hand-carved carousel, the AquaTheater, and Johnny Rockets.
Royal PromenadeThe interior main street — bars, shops, parades, and the multi-deck atrium.
Pool & Sports ZoneMultiple pools, twin FlowRider surf simulators, a zip line, mini-golf, ice rink.
Vitality Spa & FitnessFull spa, salon, and a serious gym.
Entertainment DistrictBroadway-caliber theater (Hairspray runs onboard), comedy club, jazz lounge, casino.

Plus the headline pieces — the Ultimate Abyss (a ten-story dry slide), the AquaTheater high-diving shows, and a Solarium adults-only deck. Three ports, two sea days, and a ship that can absorb six nights without feeling repetitive.

Best for: someone who wants the cruise itself to be the show — and who'll actually use the onboard amenities rather than parking on a pool deck for a week.

Carnival Cruise Line · Dream-class · Mid-to-large

Carnival Breeze

Casual, lively, food-forward — the value pick with real onboard fun

Breeze is a Dream-class Carnival ship — mid-to-large size at about 4,600 guests, built in 2012 with refurbishments since. The signature onboard experiences are WaterWorks (the Twister waterslide and a kids' splash zone), SportSquare (ropes course, mini-golf, basketball), and the Cloud 9 Spa.

Comedy is a Carnival strength: the Punchliner Comedy Club runs multiple shows nightly, including R-rated late shows. Food onboard is hearty and abundant — Guy Fieri's burger joint, BlueIguana cantina, RedFrog Pub, and the always-included Lido buffet are part of every fare. Specialty options include Cucina del Capitano (Italian) and the Steakhouse. Adults can retreat to Serenity, the adults-only sundeck at the bow.

Best for: someone who wants vacation to feel like a vacation — relaxed, food-forward, lively rather than polished. The vibe is "good party" rather than "premium resort."

Carnival Cruise Line · Dream-class namesake · Mid-to-large

Carnival Dream

Same Carnival energy as the Breeze — plus Belize, plus the Lanai outdoor walkaround

Dream is the namesake of Carnival's Dream class, built in 2009 and refurbished in 2018. It carries the same headline Carnival experiences as Breeze — WaterWorks aqua park, Cloud 9 Spa, the Punchliner Comedy Club, Guy Fieri's burgers, BlueIguana, RedFrog — but adds one signature feature the Breeze doesn't have: The Lanai, a wraparound outdoor promenade on Deck 5 lined with hammocks and Adirondack chairs that gives the ship a distinctively breezy, "walking around outside" character.

The other thing that sets these two Dream sailings apart is the itinerary — this one stops in Belize, which is the most distinct port in the entire July lineup. Belize tenders ships at the harbor for excursions to Mayan ruins (Altun Ha, Lamanai), the Belize Barrier Reef (the second-largest reef system in the world), and Caye Caulker for snorkeling.

Best for: someone who wants the casual Carnival vibe but a richer port lineup, with Belize as the highlight.

Royal Caribbean vs. Carnival

If you've never sailed both, here's the honest read on how they differ — beyond just price.
Royal Caribbean
Polished, family-broad, entertainment-heavy
  • Larger ships across the fleet — especially Oasis-class (Symphony, Wonder, Icon)
  • Higher production-value entertainment: Broadway shows, ice skating, AquaTheater on the bigger ships
  • Dining feels slightly more polished; specialty restaurants run $25–65 per person
  • Service is consistent and attentive
  • Drink packages and Wi-Fi are noticeably more expensive than Carnival
  • Crowd skews multi-generational and family-broad — more couples, fewer bachelor parties
Carnival
Casual, lively, value-focused
  • Mid-size to large ships — none reach Oasis-class scale
  • Stronger comedy programming, big casino floors, more party-friendly atmosphere
  • Food is hearty and abundant — Guy Fieri's burger joint, BlueIguana cantina, RedFrog Pub all included
  • Drink packages and add-ons price out lower than Royal Caribbean
  • Service is friendly and warm, slightly less formal
  • Crowd skews younger and more casual — louder energy, especially on shorter sailings

Neither line is "better" — they're built for different vacation moods. Royal Caribbean tends to feel like a polished resort at sea. Carnival tends to feel like a really good party with great food.
